# 05 · Learning with AI

An LLM is one of the best learning tools ever built — *if* you use it critically. Used passively, it hands you fluent answers you never actually absorb. Used well, it is a tireless tutor that meets you exactly where you are. This module is about the difference, and it is where your **learning log** earns its place.

## The trap

The easy path is: ask, read the confident answer, nod, move on. You feel like you learned something. You did not — you outsourced it. The fluency that makes LLMs feel like good teachers is the same fluency that hides what you do not understand. Learning requires *you* doing the thinking; the model's job is to provoke and check it, not replace it.

## Techniques that actually teach

Use these against your project material and the concepts you have ingested.

- **Explain it back.** After the model explains something, close it and explain it in your own words — out loud or in the learning log. Then ask the model to find the gaps. The struggle to reproduce it is where learning happens.
- **Socratic mode.** Ask the model to teach by questioning, not answering: *"Don't give me the answer. Ask me questions that lead me to it, one at a time."*
- **Make me practice.** *"Generate five questions that test whether I really understand this, hardest last."* Then answer them before checking.
- **Find my error.** Attempt something first, then: *"Here is my reasoning. Where is it wrong, and why?"* You learn far more from a corrected attempt than from a handed answer.
- **Steelman and counter.** *"Give me the strongest case for X, then the strongest case against."* Forces you past the first framing.
- **From my sources, for me.** *"Explain X using only this project's sources, at the level of someone who knows Y but not Z."* Grounded and personalized — the wiki's real superpower.

## Tie it to the failure modes

Learning with AI is where the four failure modes bite hardest:

- **Fluency ≠ correctness** → a clear explanation can still be wrong. Verify against sources.
- **Automation bias** → "the AI taught me, so it's right" is not understanding. Test yourself.
- **Anchoring** → the first explanation shapes everything after. Ask for another framing.
- **Sycophancy** → a model that praises your half-right answer teaches you nothing. Ask it to be a hard grader.

## The learning log, in practice

[`wiki/learning-log.md`](../wiki/learning-log.md) is the project log's twin. Every time you genuinely learn something, capture it **in your own words** — not a copy-paste of the model's answer. An entry that is just pasted output is a tell that you skipped the thinking.

A good entry: the concept, your explanation, what confused you at first, and an open question. Over time this log becomes a record of how *you* came to understand your project — something no amount of agent output can produce for you.

## Hands-on

1. Pick a concept central to your project that you only half-understand.
2. Run a Socratic session: have the agent question you toward it, grounded in your sources.
3. Explain it back from memory. Ask the agent to label your gaps (the eval loop, on yourself).
4. Write the corrected understanding into the learning log, in your own words, with one open question.
