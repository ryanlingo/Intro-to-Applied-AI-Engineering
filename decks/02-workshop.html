A quickstart, not a course

Intro to Applied AI Engineering

Use AI to think, document, and learn on work you actually care about.

You take a generic AI agent and turn this repo into a living LLM wiki for a project of your own: it reads your material, builds knowledge you can question, documents the work and your learning, and tells you whether to trust it.

Six short, self-paced modules. Do the hands-on steps on your own material — the goal is a wiki you keep using.

The big why

Stop using AI as an answer machine.

Ask, copy, forget. It feels productive and teaches you nothing — the fluency that makes a model sound smart is the same fluency that hides what you don't actually understand.

The alternative: build a small engine on top of your own material that helps you figure out what you don't yet know — while you stay the critical thinker in the loop.

The goal isn't answers faster. It's better thinking, documented as you go, that you can trust because you tested it.

The landscape · what it's for

What applied AI engineering helps you do.

Across almost any knowledge work, the same kind of engine helps in a handful of broad ways:

Understand

Turn a pile of docs, code, or data into knowledge you can question.

Find & ground

Get answers from your material, with citations — not generic guesses.

Create

Draft and revise documents, code, and plans; get past the blank page.

Decide

Lay out options and tradeoffs, and pressure-test your reasoning.

Learn

A tutor that explains, quizzes, and grades honestly — so it sticks.

Trust

Test whether the output is right before you rely on it.

This quickstart builds one such engine — an LLM wiki — and uses it for the foundation the rest build on: understand, learn, and trust your own material.

What you build

Three layers, two habits.

Sources
Raw material you give it — specs, docs, notes, data. The agent reads, never edits.
Wiki
The knowledge base the agent writes and keeps current: pages, an index, concepts, citations.
Schema
AGENTS.md — the rules that turn a generic agent into a disciplined wiki maintainer. You own it.

And two habits that run the whole time:

  • Document everything, on two tracks — a project log and a learning log.
  • Evaluate — a loop you run to check whether the wiki, and your own understanding, hold up.

Concepts · the landscape

Three ways to put a model to work.

Not a ranking and not a guide for action — a map of the space, so you can tell which mode you're actually in.

Prompts
→
Workflows
→
Agents
CONTEXT — what the model knows. The ground all three run on, not a mode you pick.
  • Prompts — one-off. You ask, the model answers.
  • Workflows — a fixed, predetermined program: steps you define in advance, run the same way each time.
  • Agents — dynamic: the model creates and revises its own steps at runtime to reach a goal.

Left to right, the steps shift from fixed to dynamic — and from you deciding them to the model deciding them. Every mode is only as good as the context beneath it.

Concepts · the vocabulary (1 of 2)

The words you'll use constantly.

LLM — predicts the next chunk of text. Brilliant at sounding right, which is not being right. Treat it as a fast, confident intern with no memory and no accountability.

Token — the unit a model reads, writes, and bills in. "Cheaper / fit more" = fewer tokens.

Context window — all the text the model can see at once. It's finite. What's not in the window doesn't exist to the model. Managing it is most of the skill.

Prompt — your instruction. A good one is a specification (what, in what form, with what constraints), not a magic phrase.

Context — everything the model needs before it can help: facts, files, goals, audience. It's the grounding that makes an answer about your project, not a generic one.

Concepts · the vocabulary (2 of 2)

From tools to trust.

Tool / tool-calling — a capability the model can invoke (read a file, run a command, search). Where the power and the risk live, so tools get boundaries.

Agent — an LLM in a loop with tools: read, decide, act, observe, repeat. An agent without checks is just automation with confidence.

Harness — the program that wraps the model, gives it tools, and runs the loop. Here that's opencode.

AGENTS.md — the schema that turns a generic agent into your disciplined one. The heart of this repo.

Eval — a test of whether output is actually good: a question with a known-good answer. Turns "I think it works" into a number.

Hallucination — a confident, fluent, wrong answer. The default failure of LLMs. The whole discipline exists to catch it.

Concepts · what you're hunting

The four failure modes.

Name them when you catch them — they're baked into AGENTS.md so the agent self-checks too.

Failure modeWhat it looks like
Fluency ≠ correctnessIt sounds right. Check whether it is.
Automation bias"A machine made it" is not evidence it's true.
AnchoringThe first framing or first source isn't automatically the best one.
SycophancyA model that agrees with you isn't the same as a model that's right.

Setup · safety & checkpoints

Keep a known-good state. Verify before moving on.

Rules

  • Never paste secrets or private data into a prompt or source file.
  • Checkpoint before letting the agent change anything (git commit, or dated copies).
  • Review every change before you accept it.
  • Prefer small, reviewable steps.
  • Give the agent narrow authority — let it own wiki/, not your machine.

Smoke test

Read AGENTS.md and tell me, in your own words, what your job is in this repo and what you are not allowed to touch.

If it answers that it maintains wiki/, follows the schema, and must never edit sources/ — your setup works.

The wiki pattern.

Instead of asking the model from memory, you give it a folder of your material and let it build and maintain a knowledge base on top.

AGENTS.md · the rules

Why Markdown? A .md file opens in any editor, on any system, with no vendor lock-in. Your knowledge doesn't depend on a company staying in business or an app keeping a feature. It's yours, in plain text, forever.

Document & wiki · two tracks

Document everything — on two logs.

TrackFileCapturesAnswers
Projectwiki/log.mdWhat was done, what changed, decisions and why, what's open.What happened to the work?
Learningwiki/learning-log.mdWhat you understood, in your own words; aha-moments; open questions.What did I figure out?

The project log is the memory of the work. The learning log is the memory of you getting smarter. AGENTS.md tells the agent to keep both: it appends to the project log after work, and prompts you for a learning entry whenever it teaches you something.

Document & wiki · hands-on

Build your wiki — on real material.

  • Add sources. Drop 2–3 real files into sources/ — a spec, a design doc, data, an article, notes.
  • Ingest:
Ingest the files in sources/. For each, create a summary page in the wiki and extract the key concepts and entities into their own pages, with source citations. Then update wiki/index.md and log what you did.
  • Inspect. Open the new pages. Does every claim cite a source? Did it mark anything unverified? In Obsidian, check the graph.
  • Ask a grounded question only your sources can answer. A good answer cites files and names the gaps; one that confidently fills gaps is a hallucination — note it.
  • Check the logs. Confirm log.md got an entry; write your first learning-log.md entry in your own words.

If the agent edited a file in sources/, stop and tighten AGENTS.md — that fix-the-rules move is how you train your agent.

Evaluation · the loop

Trust you test, not trust you hope for.

A wiki you can't trust is worse than no wiki — it's confident misinformation.

Re-test

"Looks good" is fluency talking. An eval set turns a vibe into a number: of my ten test questions, the wiki gets eight grounded and right. Now you can prove an improvement instead of guessing at one.

It's a loop, not a one-time check. Every failure you catch makes the next version more trustworthy.

Evaluation · hands-on

Build your eval set, then label what fails.

The run

  • Write 5–10 questions your wiki should answer from the sources. Mix easy with hard (where do two sources disagree?).
  • Write the known-good answer yourself, from the sources.
  • Run each in a fresh context; record the answer next to yours.
  • Improve — usually a sharper AGENTS.md rule. Smallest fix that addresses the cause.
  • Re-test and log the before/after.

Label each miss

  • ungrounded — claimed something no source supports
  • wrong — contradicted by a source
  • incomplete — missed something the sources cover
  • anchored — over-relied on one source
  • sycophantic — agreed with a leading question

Point the same loop at yourself: explain a concept without looking, have the agent label your failures against the sources, record the corrected version.

Learning with AI · the throughline

A tireless tutor — if you use it critically.

The trap: ask, read the confident answer, nod, move on. You feel like you learned something. You didn't — you outsourced it.

Learning requires you doing the thinking; the model's job is to provoke and check it, not replace it.

  • Explain it back. Close the answer, reproduce it in your own words, then ask the model to find the gaps.
  • Socratic mode. "Don't give me the answer. Ask me questions that lead me to it, one at a time."
  • Make me practice. "Generate five questions that test whether I really understand this, hardest last."
  • Find my error. Attempt first, then "Here's my reasoning — where is it wrong, and why?"
  • Steelman and counter. Strongest case for X, then the strongest against — past the first framing.
  • From my sources, for me. Explain X using only this project's sources, at my level.

Learning with AI · the log

The learning log, in practice.

Every time you genuinely learn something, capture it in your own words — not a copy-paste of the model's answer. Pasted output is a tell that you skipped the thinking.

A good entry has: the concept · your explanation · what confused you at first · one open question.

Over time this log becomes a record of how you came to understand your project — something no amount of agent output can produce for you.

This is where the four failure modes bite hardest. A clear explanation can still be wrong; verify against sources. A model that praises your half-right answer teaches you nothing; ask it to be a hard grader.

Put it to work · the capstone

Point the whole engine at a real problem.

Take a genuine problem — a bug you don't understand, a decision you're stuck on, material you need to make sense of — and work it through end to end. Run every pillar at least once:

Ingest
Put the real material into sources/; let the agent build the wiki.
Direct
Ask grounded questions. Use the lowest rung that works.
Document
Keep both logs current — what you did, and what you learned.
Evaluate
Run your eval set. Trust answers only after you've tested them.
Learn
Use the agent to actually understand the problem, not just resolve it.
Decide
Make the call. Write the decision and why into the project log.

Put it to work · the standard

Done = a small engine you trust.

  • A working wiki on your real problem: sources ingested, pages linked, index.md current.
  • A decision or result you can defend, with the reasoning recorded in wiki/log.md.
  • An eval run with failures labeled and at least one AGENTS.md improvement that came from them.
  • A learning-log entry in your own words: what you understand now that you didn't, and the next question you'd chase.
  • An AGENTS.md that is genuinely yours — edited from experience, not the starter defaults.

You're judged on restraint and honesty, not cleverness. A plain prompt that solved it beats an elaborate agent that impressed.
